Overview

The mule deer is a North American deer species known for its large ears and distinctive bounding leap called stotting or pronging.
Mule deer are commonly found in western North America and are named after their mule-like ears.

Scientific name

Odocoileus hemionus

Key identification features

  • Large, mule-like ears
  • Forked antlers on males (usually)
  • White rump patch with a black-tipped tail

Habitat & Range

Mule deer occupy a variety of habitats including open woodlands, shrublands, and mountainous regions.

Geographic range

They are widespread across western Canada, the western United States, and into parts of Mexico.

Preferred environments

  • Mixed conifer and aspen stands
  • Sagebrush scrub
  • Subalpine meadows (seasonally)

Diet

Mule deer are primarily browsers — they feed on leaves, twigs, herbs, and shrubs. During the winter they rely more on woody browse.

Typical foods

  • Sagebrush and bitterbrush
  • Forbs and grasses (seasonally)
  • Shrub foliage and young twigs
Foraging behavior

They often feed during dawn and dusk (crepuscular activity) and will shift feeding locations with seasonal plant availability.

Behavior & Life Cycle

Reproduction

The rut (breeding season) typically occurs in the fall. Does give birth in late spring to early summer, usually to one or two fawns.

Social structure

Mule deer may form small groups (does and fawns) and males can be solitary outside of the rut.

Anti-predator adaptations

They use a sharp bounding leap and excellent hearing to evade predators. Fawns use camouflage and remain motionless when threatened.
